Jane Austen (1775–1817) wrote about drawing rooms, dances, and “proper introductions”… and somehow ended up writing some of the sharpest relationship and status commentary in English literature. Her world is small on purpose: by zooming in on manners, money, and marriage, she shows how power works—quietly, socially, daily.

This post is a timeline of Austen’s life and the books we have on **Podcast library**, with links to listen as you go.

## Quick timeline: Austen’s life in a few dates

- **1775**: Born in Steventon, Hampshire, England.
- **1790s**: Writes early stories, experiments with voice and satire, and drafts the first versions of several later novels.
- **1801**: The family moves to Bath—Austen writes less, but keeps revising.
- **1809**: Settles in Chawton (a village near Alton). This is the productive stretch where she revises and completes the novels we now know best.
- **1811–1815**: Publishes her major novels during her lifetime (anonymously: “By a Lady”).
- **1817**: Dies at 41. Two novels are published posthumously that same year.

## The six major novels (what they’re about, when she wrote them, why they still hit)

### Sense and Sensibility (written in the 1790s; published 1811)

Listen: [Sense and Sensibility](/shows/sense-and-sensibility-by-jane-austen-5684539)

**What it’s about**: Two sisters respond to heartbreak and social pressure in opposite ways—Elinor with restraint (sense), Marianne with open emotion (sensibility).  
**Why it matters today**: It’s basically a study of coping styles—how people perform “being fine,” how families distribute emotional labor, and how money quietly controls choices.

### Pride and Prejudice (first drafted in the 1790s; published 1813)

Listen: [Pride and Prejudice](/shows/pride-and-prejudice-jane-austen-novel-5717814)

**What it’s about**: Elizabeth Bennet vs. her own first impressions—plus the most famous slow-burn tension in classic fiction.  
**Why it matters today**: It’s a masterclass in misreading people, social media-level snap judgments, and the work it takes to revise your own story about someone.

### Mansfield Park (written 1811–1813; published 1814)

Listen: [Mansfield Park](/shows/mansfield-park-by-jane-austen-5680641)

**What it’s about**: Fanny Price grows up inside a wealthy household where morality and charm don’t always line up.  
**Why it matters today**: It asks a hard question: when you’re the “guest” in a system you didn’t build, what do you owe it—and what do you owe yourself?

### Emma (written 1814–1815; published 1815)

Listen: [Emma](/shows/emma-jane-austen-5509443)

**What it’s about**: A privileged matchmaker keeps getting it wrong—until she finally sees her own blind spots.  
**Why it matters today**: It’s about narrative control: how someone with influence can shape other people’s lives while telling themselves it’s “helping.”

### Persuasion (written 1815–1816; published 1817)

Listen: [Persuasion](/shows/persuasion-by-jane-austen-5690549)

**What it’s about**: Anne Elliot gets a second chance at the love she was talked out of years earlier.  
**Why it matters today**: It’s for anyone who’s ever wondered what their life would look like if they’d trusted themselves earlier—and what it means to start again without being 19.

### Northanger Abbey (written in the 1790s; published 1817)

Listen: [Northanger Abbey](/shows/northanger-abbey-by-jane-austen-5767446)

**What it’s about**: A young woman raised on Gothic thrillers expects dark secrets everywhere… and learns what real-world manipulation looks like instead.  
**Why it matters today**: It’s about media literacy before media literacy had a name: how stories shape expectations, and how to tell drama from danger.

## Why Austen stays relevant (even if you don’t care about “Regency”)

Austen’s settings are period-specific, but her engines are modern:

- **Status anxiety**: who gets invited, who gets respected, who gets dismissed.
- **Money as plot**: the reality that “romance” is never just romance when rent is involved.
- **Communication gaps**: what people mean vs. what they can safely say out loud.
- **The comedy of self-deception**: characters lie to themselves in ways that feel… painfully current.

If you’re new to Austen, start with [Pride and Prejudice](/shows/pride-and-prejudice-jane-austen-novel-5717814) for the most instantly addictive arc, or [Persuasion](/shows/persuasion-by-jane-austen-5690549) if you want something quieter and more mature.
